1. Introduction

Total knee replacement (TKR), also known as total knee arthroplasty, is one of the most successful and commonly performed orthopaedic procedures worldwide. It is done to relieve pain and restore function in patients suffering from knee osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or post traumatic joint degeneration. TKR has evolved a lot in the past years. There have been advances in implant design, surgical robots, and also navigation systems, but the end goal has always been to achieve optimal soft tissue balancing to ensure accurate and enhanced surgical outcomes. Intraoperative soft tissue tension directly influences joint kinematics, stability, and patient satisfaction, and imbalance in these tissues often leads to complications such as pain, stiffness, instability, or early implant failure.

Intraoperative soft tissue tension, especially in the medial collateral ligament (MCL), lateral collateral ligament (LCL), and posterior cruciate ligament (PCL), exert a lot of influence on the biomechanics of the joint, and the kinematics and longevity of the implant. When there is an imbalance present in the knee, patients may experience persistent pain, joint instability or stiffness, dissatisfaction, premature wear of the prosthetic, and early revision surgery. This is a major challenge in achieving good TKR outcomes. With hundreds of thousands of TKR procedures performed annually around the world, the clinical and economic stakes are high, thus making soft tissue balancing not just a technical nuance but a core determinant of success.

1.1 Background

The knee is a complex join comprising of the distal femur (thigh bone), the proximal tibia (shin bone), and the patella (knee cap). The 2 main bones are the femur and tibia (as shown in image 1). They are covered by cartilage and supported by menisci. Knee stability is provided by the three main ligaments (MCL, LCL, PCL). In a healthy, non-diseased knee, these structures work together to allow flexion and extension of the knee with balanced ligament tension and proportionate load distribution (MBA, 2019).

When degenerative disease such as osteoarthritis alters this anatomy, the knee becomes painful, stiff, deformed (varus or valgus), and mobility becomes limited. At the point where conservative therapies (medications, injections, physical therapy, and lifestyle modification) no longer provide relief, TKR becomes unavoidable.

TKR is most commonly performed in adults between 45 and 80 years old, which is the age range in which degenerative joint disease becomes symptomatic and limits functionality. The procedure is notably more common in females, who have a higher prevalence of osteoarthritis due to anatomical, hormonal, and biomechanical factors. Additional high-risk groups include patients with diabetes, obesity, and those with a family history of osteoarthritis, all of which substantially increase cartilage degeneration and worsen joint inflammation. Patients with a history of previous knee injuries—such as ligament tears, fractures, or meniscal damage—also tend to develop earlier-onset post-traumatic arthritis, often requiring TKR at a younger age. These demographic trends highlight the broad and growing population that depends on successful knee replacement outcomes.

The TKR procedure removes the damaged bone surfaces (as seen in Image: 03) and replaces them with prosthetic components (implants), to restore alignment and stability. These implants come in various sizes depending on the patient’s physicality and deformity severity.

1.2 Significance

Soft tissue tensioning refers to the process of balancing the ligaments in the knee during TKR to ensure that the alignment outcomes post-surgery are as similar to the native knee of the patient pre-surgery. In the workflow above, steps 4, 7, and 9 aim to achieve this soft tissue balancing through knee assessment.

One of the most critical determinants of successful outcomes in TKR is the achievement of proper soft tissue balance. During surgery, the medial collateral ligament (MCL), lateral collateral ligament (LCL), and posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) must be tensioned appropriately to ensure joint stability and natural kinematics throughout the range of motion. However, current balancing techniques are largely subjective and rely on the surgeon’s tactile feedback and experience. This dependence introduces significant variability between surgeons and cases, contributing to inconsistent outcomes and a steep learning curve for less experienced surgeons.

Emerging technologies such as computer-assisted navigation and robotic systems have attempted to address this variability, yet they often lack real-time quantitative data on soft tissue tension. Consequently, there remains a pressing clinical need for an intraoperative solution that can provide objective, real-time feedback on ligament forces to help surgeons achieve personalized and reproducible soft tissue balance.

1.2.1 Clinical Significance

The clinical significance of soft tissue balancing in TKR cannot be overstated. Surgeons currently manage knee balance by using a variety of alignment strategies:

  • Mechanical alignment, aiming to restore a neutral mechanical axis of the limb, i.e., restore a straight knee with 0° deviation from the axis.
  • Kinematic alignment, attempting to respect the patient’s native joint line and soft tissue envelope. This has proved to have the best outcomes due to the importance given to soft tissue balancing in restoring the native knee.
  • Hybrid approaches, combining elements of both depending on patient anatomy or surgeon philosophy.

During surgery, knee assessment for soft tissue balancing usually involves the surgeon applying a varus-valgus stress in order to check the gaps in the medial and lateral compartment. They check the laxity in each side, and may perform some soft tissue release if they feel it is necessary. However, this process is largely based on the surgeon’s tactile experience, visual cues, and judgement, and hence experience and training is extremely important in TKR cases.

Proper soft tissue balance is fundamental to (Wu et al., 2025):

  • Joint stability: An unbalanced knee may feel unstable or give way during daily activities.
  • Even load distribution: Asymmetrical loading of the implant and bone can increase wear and tear.
  • Assess medial and lateral compartment laxities: Lateral compartment has more laxity than the medial compartment, which leads to different gap values for each compartment.
  • Range of motion and function: Over-tight ligaments may restrict flexion, and overly lax ligaments may reduce extension or cause instability.
  • Patient comfort and satisfaction: Many patients report dissatisfaction due to persistent pain, stiffness, or a “foreign” feeling knee even when the implant is well aligned.
  • Long-term implant survival: Studies suggest soft tissue imbalance may account for a significant proportion of early revisions.

Given that 10-20 % of patients remain dissatisfied (Bourne et al., 2010) and many early failures may be linked to soft tissue imbalance, improving the reproducibility and quantification of soft tissue tension intra-operatively is critical for improving outcomes.

1.2.2 Market Significance

Given the ageing global population, rising incidence of obesity, and higher functional demands of patients, the volume of TKR continues to escalate. Currently there are about 3.6 million TKR surgeries (Deshpande et al., 2016) done worldwide per year (Leung et al., 2018).



2. Problem Clarification

Despite the high procedural success and widespread adoption of TKR, the patient satisfaction rates are still considered to be significantly low. TKR is reported to have only 80% satisfaction rates (Bourne et al., 2010), which amounts to about 660,000 dissatisfied patients yearly. Total Hip Replacement (THR) is a surgery that is done in a very similar method to TKR, yet, the satisfaction for THR were reported to be 90%. This discrepancy occurs due to a number of factors, however, the main contributor is the challenge of intraoperative soft tissue balancing in knee replacement.

Reasons for low satisfaction rates after TKR (Dhrupad Ponnamaneni et al., 2024):

  • Poor knee alignment due to soft tissue imbalance: Under-tensioning or over-tensioning the ligaments leads to instability, stiffness, or unnatural knee kinematics, which makes the joint feel unstable or “not like my own.” Even small imbalances can significantly affect pain, function, and long-term implant success. (35% of total dissatisfaction cases; shown in pie chart below) (Sun et al., 2022)
  • Unrealistic expectations: Some patients anticipate a completely “normal” knee or rapid recovery, leading to disappointment when outcomes don’t match expectations. Mismatch between perceived and actual functional improvement is a major driver of dissatisfaction.
  • Psychological and social factors: Anxiety, depression, low pain tolerance, or inadequate social support can negatively influence perceived recovery and overall satisfaction. These factors often magnify discomfort and slow functional progress.
  • Post-surgery complications: Issues such as infection, stiffness, persistent pain, or instability reduce trust in the surgical outcome. Even minor complications can significantly lower satisfaction despite technically successful surgery.
  • Post-surgery care: Incomplete rehabilitation, poor adherence to physiotherapy, or inadequate follow-up can delay functional recovery. Insufficient guidance during the recovery period often contributes more to dissatisfaction than the surgery itself.

2.1. Clinical Challenge

It has been established that the main reason for dissatisfaction is improper soft tissue balancing during surgery. This implies that the current surgical procedure has some major shortcomings.

Issues with current TKR procedure:

  • Knee assessment is very surgeon-guided and subjective (Corbett et al., 2025):
    • Soft tissue balancing relies heavily on a surgeon’s tactile experience, visual inspection, and “feel,” leading to significant variability between surgeons.
    • Different surgeons apply different magnitudes of force when testing ligament tension, making outcomes inconsistent and difficult to standardize.
    • Even within the same surgeon, force application can vary across procedures due to fatigue, perception, or intraoperative conditions.


  • No personalised system to determine the ideal force:
    • Every patient’s knee ligament stiffness is unique, and thus the ideal amount of force applied to every knee is also unique.
    • There cannot be one force value that can be applied to every knee; a personalised system is required to determine each patient’s “ideal force.”


  • No measurable and reproducible force applied during gap assessment:
    • Manual force application creates wide variability in measured gaps. The same knee may appear “balanced” or “unbalanced” depending on the force used.
    • Without objective force control, surgeons cannot reliably reproduce the same conditions across the extension and flexion arcs or between knees.
  • Existing tensioner devices disrupt workflow and lack compatibility:
    • They add additional steps to the workflow, which is inconvenient for the surgeon.
    • Digital tensioning devices available today are typically tied to specific robotic or navigation platforms, limiting their use to certain systems and institutions.

2.3. Interview Insights

We conducted 35 interviews with orthopaedic surgeons and industry professionals, and there was strong consensus across most responses. We asked questions to gain insights on soft tissue balancing and personalised force in gap assessment. Most surgeons agreed that achieving proper soft tissue balance remains one of the most subjective and variable aspects of TKR.

All surgeons acknowledged that balanced ligaments are critical for joint stability and patient satisfaction. However, there is currently no standardized method for quantifying soft tissue tension intra-operatively. Most rely on tactile “feel,” visual cues, and personal experience to judge when tension is “just right.” They emphasized that this feel-based method causes significant variability in outcomes.

The surgeons used different alignment approaches — mechanical alignment, kinematic alignment, or hybrid alignment — depending on patient anatomy and case complexity. Several surgeons noted that poor soft tissue balancing, rather than misalignment alone, is a major cause of postoperative stiffness, instability, and patient dissatisfaction. Most preferred robotic TKR over conventional TKR due to improved accuracy and faster surgeries.

When asked about balance assessment tools, most surgeons still rely on conventional instruments such as laminar spreaders, spacer blocks, and gap balancers, while only a few use force sensors or digital tensioners. All interviewees agreed that there is currently no gold standard for measuring ligament tension intra-operatively.

Several expressed interest in a quantitative, real-time feedback system to improve reproducibility, though others cautioned that soft tissue balance is inherently patient- and surgeon-specific. However, the consensus was that if a device could quantify varus–valgus force, it would not only improve patient satisfaction but also reduce the learning curve for junior surgeons through increased standardization.

2.4. Key Pain Points

Based on our interview and survey findings, we narrowed down the problem to the following pain points for surgeons:

  1. No personalised force: This was a major concern across most surgeons. Every patient's knee is different, and every surgeon applies a different force when assessing knee gaps. Currently, no one knows the “ideal force” for each knee. This “ideal force” is patient specific, and varies based on the patient's BMI and deformity severity. There is no current way to know or measure this personalized force.
  2. Lack of objectivity: Surgeons’ body type and strength vary, so the force applied during gap assessment is inconsistent. For example, a petite surgeon may apply less force than a larger surgeon. Force is currently based on “feel,” and standardizing it is necessary for consistency in gap assessment.
  3. Lack of replicability: No device currently enables reproducible force application. Once the force is objectified based on patient specificity, it can be consistently replicated when testing implants. This reproducible force will better mimic the native knee environment before bone cuts and enable improved gap balancing.
  4. Low patient satisfaction: Satisfaction for Total Hip Replacement is 90%, while Total Knee Replacement is only 80%. Surgeons believe improving soft tissue balancing can help increase satisfaction rates.
  5. Long training hours: Measuring soft tissue tension could reduce the learning curve for junior surgeons performing TKR, allowing them to learn faster and more consistently.

2.5 Needs Statement

After analysing the problem, our project aims:

To develop a method to produce a personalised gap (±0.5 mm) intra-operatively during primary Total Knee Replacement, based on native lateral and medial cruciate ligament tension (50–250N), empowering surgeons to achieve reproducible balance, allowing junior surgeons to shorten their learning curve, and enabling patients to experience more natural alignment, thereby improving patient satisfaction (90%).

4.5. Concept Logic

Through all the concepts shown above, although there are many possible embodiments for the different functional requirements, this section focuses more on the “algorithm” functional requirement to better understand the logic that our device follows. We established that there are 3 main requirements: measure force, measure displacement, cue the surgeon when ideal force has been reached. In order to do this, we have two ideas as explained below.

4.5.1. Force Displacement Curve (Logic 1)

This concept works on the basis of a force-displacement curve. Ligaments in the knee behave like elastic materials, meaning they stretch under load and follow predictable elastic behaviour until they reach a point of yielding. When a ligament is gradually tensioned, the relationship between the applied force and the resulting displacement forms a force–displacement curve. In the early region of the curve, the ligament stretches elastically, and force increases gradually with displacement. As the ligament approaches its elastic limit, the curve begins to flatten, leading to a point of inflection, also known as the yielding point. Beyond this point, the ligament enters the plastic region where permanent damage or over-stretching can occur (Chantarapanich Beng et al., 2009).

The goal of soft-tissue balancing is to tension the ligaments safely within their elastic region, without allowing the surgeon to approach or exceed the yielding point. The “ideal force” to be applied by a surgeon is considered to be approximately 10% before the yielding point force. This ensures that the ligament is sufficiently tensioned for stability, but not overstressed. When the surgeon reaches this optimal force threshold, the device will provide an immediate signal to stop applying additional force.

Since ligament stiffness and elasticity vary widely between individuals (Readioff et al., 2020), the force–displacement curve will be different for every patient. By measuring both force and displacement intra-operatively, our device can generate each patient’s personalised curve in real time. This creates a customised, objective feedback system that guides the surgeon to the optimal tension unique to that patient, improving precision and reducing risks associated with over- or under-tensioning.

However, this method has some risks and concerns:

  • Due to the complex biomechanics of the knee, the graph will not be perfectly linear before the point of inflection. How do we go about finding the yield point if the slope is changing even before the point of inflection? (Buhary et al., 2019)
  • In order to know the force value 10% before the yielding point, we need to know the yielding point. However, if the force reaches the yielding point that would mean the ligament has already crossed its maximum limit. Is this damage significant?
  • We have some preliminary answers based on surgeon feedback, which suggests that it is acceptable to go just beyond the yield point because it is considered very mild damage. This is something we hope to investigate further before reaching a proper conclusion.

4.5.2. Balance Solver Method (Logic 2)

Another method we explored for determining the optimal soft-tissue tension is the Balance Solver Method, which leverages the elastic behaviour of ligaments under loading. When a controlled force is applied to a ligament, it elongates, but once the force is removed, the ligament returns to its original length — as long as the force remains within the elastic region. This reversible stretch continues until the ligament approaches its yielding point.

However, when the applied force exceeds this yielding point, the ligament enters the plastic region. In this region, the tissue does not fully return to its original length after the force is released, indicating a small but measurable amount of permanent stretch. This irreversible change becomes a useful marker: it reveals that the applied force has crossed the safe limit for that ligament.

The Balance Solver Method uses this principle by incrementally applying tension and then releasing it to observe whether the ligament fully recoils. The point at which the ligament fails to return to its original length is identified as the yielding point. Once this threshold is detected, the system calculates the ideal force as 10% below the yielding force, consistent with established soft-tissue safety guidelines.

By repeatedly applying and releasing small controlled forces, the Balance Solver Method can determine the yielding point without overstressing the ligament and without requiring continuous high-force loading. This enables a safe, personalised, and objective measurement of each patient’s optimal tension level and provides a clear signal to the surgeon when the ideal force is reached.

Moving forward, we will test logic 1 through our prototyping plan, but if some risks and limitations mentioned above arise, we might have to try prototyping with logic 2 as well.

5.2. Design 1: Mechanical Jack with Screw

The first concept to be prototyped employs a mechanical jack integrated with a lead-screw system to generate a force–displacement curve and identify the ligament’s “ideal force.” The lead screw enables precise control, where each rotation corresponds to a defined change in the joint gap, ensuring consistent and reproducible displacement measurements. The jack applies a smooth, evenly distributed force across the joint, preventing sudden jumps or uneven loading. Together, this setup allows accurate correlation between applied force and displacement, forming the basis for a reliable and repeatable force–displacement curve.

5.4. Design 3: Inflatables

Another proposed design explores the use of an inflatable actuator to generate controlled force within the knee joint. This concept also relies on the force–displacement curve to determine the “ideal force,” but replaces mechanical springs with a pressurised inflatable element. Since both the contact area of the inflatable and the internal pressure are known, the applied force can be calculated directly using F=P×A, allowing for precise and continuous force measurement.

Displacement will be captured using the same sensor-based approach as in other concepts, with the option of integrating a Vernier calliper-style mechanism if higher measurement accuracy is required. 5.5. Testing Plan & Roadmap

Moving forward, after we make the different prototypes we also aim to create a testing environment. The testing plan is structured in four progressive stages to ensure accuracy, safety, and clinical validity of the device. It begins with bench top calibration to verify sensor accuracy and graph generation, followed by pig knee testing to assess physiological realism and tissue safety. The cadaveric trial stage focuses on validating reproducibility and surgeon feedback in realistic surgical conditions. Finally, a clinical pilot will evaluate usability, surgeon correlation, and postoperative outcomes, ensuring the system’s readiness for real-world application.
